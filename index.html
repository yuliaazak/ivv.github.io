Policy

The increase of domestic full-cycle production of wine remained a governmental target in Russia. In 2016, several new legislative changes came into force aimed at supporting the development of domestic wine production. First of all, in 2016, people were permitted to start up micro-wineries, the licence of which is more than 12 times cheaper than it is for larger wineries aiming to promote the development of domestic small wineries. Second, to ensure fair competition, a minimum legal retail price was set for sparkling wine at the level of RUB164 per 0.75 litre. The share of low-quality low-price sparkling wine based on imported grape must using artificial colours and flavours is high enough, harming full-cycle wine producers. Finally, in 2016 differentiated excise tax was applied to wine made from Russian grape and for the remaining wines including imported wines and wine made domestically but using imported grape must. The difference between excise tax on domestic wine and the rest of wine is notable to support high-quality domestic wine production in the country and viticulture, as well.

Governmental support to the development of viticulture in Russia is planned to be prolonged in the long-term. In particular, it was announced in 2017 that the government plans to invest approximately RUB48 billion over the coming eight years to significantly increase the area of vineyards, bringing them up to 125,000 hectares. To support domestic producers, there was a discussion to allow advertisements for domestically produced wine fully produced from domestically cultivated grapes, which can notably increase consumers’ interest in wine over the forecast period.
